[FINAL_REPLY_TO_USER]
Blind signing occurs when a hardware wallet signs a transaction without being able to clearly display and verify the exact details on its screen. The user presses approve without seeing the recipient address, amount, or contract details — trusting the software (like Claude Code) to have shown them accurately.

You should NOT enable blind signing if your hardware wallet offers a choice. Here's why:

1. **Device verification is your last defense.** If malicious code or a compromised MCP tries to swap the recipient address or inflate the amount at the bytes level, your Ledger screen should display the REAL values. You verify them on-device and reject if they don't match what you expect.

2. **Blind signing breaks that defense.** If the device can't display the details (or you skip reading them), you can't catch a substitution attack — you just press approve and broadcast a transaction to the wrong address or for the wrong amount.

3. **Vaultpilot's stance:** We design every signing flow so your Ledger CAN clear-sign — meaning it decodes and displays the transaction details on-device. Before approving ANY transaction, always:
   - Look at the recipient address on your Ledger screen — not your monitor.
   - Verify the amount matches what you intended.
   - Check the chain ID and token contract.
   - Only then press the physical approve button on the device.

If you ever see a prompt like "blind signing required" or "can't decode on device," that's a red flag. It means either the tool isn't well-designed, the MCP has a gap, or something is wrong. Refuse and report it.

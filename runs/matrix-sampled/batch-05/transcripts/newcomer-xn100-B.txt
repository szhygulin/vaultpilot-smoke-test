[FINAL_REPLY_TO_USER]
Discord verification messages carry inherent risk depending on what they actually sign. Here's what you should know:

**The Safe Path:**
- If it's a simple plaintext message like "I own this wallet" signed with EIP-191 (personal_sign), that's generally safe — it can't be replayed on-chain.
- Always READ the full message text before approving it on your hardware wallet screen.

**The Risky Path:**
- If Discord asks you to sign something that looks truncated, encoded, or has a long hex string, that's a red flag. Scammers sometimes hide drainer smart contracts or ERC-20 approval grants inside these messages.
- Some attackers craft messages that appear benign in wallets that don't decode the full intent but contain hidden contract interaction bytecode.

**What to Do:**
1. On your Ledger/Trezor, read EXACTLY what the device shows.
2. If you see anything about "approve," "swap," "transfer," or "permit," do NOT sign it.
3. If the message text in your wallet doesn't match what Discord claimed you were signing, abort immediately.

Right now, I don't have the ability to cryptographically verify the full intent of a typed-data message (like EIP-712 Permit grants), so you're relying on:
- The device's clear-sign display.
- Your own reading comprehension.

For Discord specifically: legitimate verification messages are simple. If it feels complex, it probably is — and "complex" on Discord usually means phishing.

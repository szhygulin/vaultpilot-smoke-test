[FINAL_REPLY_TO_USER]
Blind signing means your hardware wallet signs a transaction without displaying its full details on the device screen—it only shows a hash or summary. Clear signing means the device decodes and displays the transaction type, recipient, amount, and other key fields in human-readable form before you approve.

Why this matters: With blind signing enabled, you trust that the computer/app is honestly showing you what the transaction contains, because the device itself cannot verify it. This is the highest-risk mode because malware on the computer could fabricate a fake "Ethereum transfer" prompt while secretly authorizing a Permit or Contract Interaction.

Best practice: **Disable blind signing if your hardware wallet firmware supports clear signing for your transaction type.** Modern Ledger devices can clear-sign most EVM transactions, Solana transfers, and Bitcoin transactions. If clear signing is available, use it—the device will refuse transactions it can't decode, which is a strong defense.

When you might need blind signing: Some advanced DeFi transactions (e.g., Complex smart contract interactions) may not have device app support yet. In those cases, the computer's display becomes your verification surface—make sure you trust the software and double-check recipients and amounts before pressing approve on the device.

The strongest protection: Hardware wallet + clear-sign-capable device app + offline verification of contract addresses + never enable blind signing for high-value operations.
